# Clinical Photography Best Practices for WHO SkinNTD AI
## Guidelines for High-Quality Image Collection

These guidelines are synthesized from global clinical photography standards (BMJ, Cureus, JCAS) specifically tailored for the **WHO SkinNTD AI Image Collection Initiative**. Consistent, high-quality images are critical for training accurate and equitable AI diagnostic tools.

---

## 1. Ethical & Legal Foundation
- **Informed Consent**: Always obtain and document specific consent for AI training. Explain the purpose clearly to the patient.
- **Strict Anonymization**: Irreversibly remove all identifiers (faces, names, tattoos, jewelry, hospital logos).
- **Data Security**: Disable personal cloud backups (iCloud/Google Photos) on devices used for photography. Never share images via social media or unencrypted messaging apps.

## 2. The "Rule of Three" (Capture Strategy)
Always capture at least three images per case to provide full context:
1. **Overview (Regional)**: Shows the anatomical location (e.g., the whole limb) to orient the viewer.
2. **Contextual**: Includes surrounding healthy skin and a nearby anatomical landmark (e.g., elbow, navel).
3. **Macro (Close-up)**: Specifically details the lesion morphology at high resolution.

## 3. Focus & Technical Execution
- **Avoid Digital Zoom**: Do not pinch-to-zoom. Move the camera physically back to allow the lens to focus (usually >10cm), then crop the high-resolution image later if needed.
- **Tap to Focus**: Always tap the screen on the lesion to lock both focus and exposure on the area of interest.
- **Stabilization**: Brace your arms against your body or a hard surface to prevent motion blur.
- **Perpendicular Angle**: Always shoot straight-on to the lesion. Angled shots cause perspective distortion that misrepresents size and shape.

## 4. Lighting & Color Accuracy
- **Natural Light**: Best captured in diffuse sunlight (near a window, but not in direct harsh glare).
- **Avoid Mixed Lighting**: Do not mix yellow indoor lights with white window light; this confuses the camera's white balance.
- **Flash Usage**: Use only as a last resort. If used, try to diffuse the light (e.g., placing a thin tissue over the flash) to reduce harsh highlights/hotspots.

## 5. Environment & Framing
- **Neutral Background**: Use solid, non-reflective blue, green, or white backgrounds (e.g., a sterile towel or surgical drape). This helps the camera accurately process skin tones.
- **Clear Distractions**: Remove gloves, medical tubing, cotton swabs, or clothing from the frame before shooting.
- **Reference of Scale**: Place a standardized ruler or a common object (like a coin) adjacent to the lesion to provide a visual size reference.

## 6. Device Configuration
- **Resolution**: Set to the highest JPEG quality available.
- **Disable Filters**: Turn off "Beauty Mode," "Scene Optimizer," or "AI Enhancements" which artificially smooth skins or alter colors.
- **Turn Off "Live Photo"**: Use standard still images to save storage and ensure compatibility.
