# Module 3: Informed Consent Procedures
## Comprehensive Content Document

### Learning Objectives
By the end of this module, participants will be able to:
1. Explain the elements of valid informed consent
2. Navigate language barriers appropriately
3. Recognize signs that consent may not be voluntary
4. Respond appropriately to withdrawal of consent
5. Apply consent principles to vulnerable populations

---

## Section 1: Understanding Informed Consent

### What is Informed Consent?

Informed consent is a process, not just a signature. It requires:

**Information:**
- Purpose of the project explained clearly
- What will happen to their images
- How data will be used and protected
- Potential risks and benefits
- Voluntary nature emphasized

**Comprehension:**
- Patient understands the explanation
- Questions are answered
- Concepts are clear (not just nodded-through)
- Language is appropriate for patient's level

**Voluntariness:**
- No coercion or pressure
- No undue influence
- Freedom to say no
- Freedom to withdraw at any time

**Capacity:**
- Patient can understand and decide
- Not impaired by illness, medication, or distress
- Adult (or appropriate assent for children)

### The Consent Process

**Step 1: Introduction**
- Introduce yourself and your role
- Explain you're part of a WHO project
- Ask if they have time to talk
- Ensure privacy for discussion

**Step 2: Explanation**
- What: "We are collecting photos of skin conditions"
- Why: "To train computers to help diagnose leprosy"
- How: "Your photos will be used anonymously"
- Protection: "Your identity will be protected"

**Step 3: Questions and Answers**
- Invite questions
- Answer honestly
- Don't rush
- Check understanding

**Step 4: Documentation**
- Provide consent form
- Allow time to read (or read to them)
- Address concerns
- Obtain signature/documentation

**Step 5: Ongoing Consent**
- Remind them they can stop anytime
- Check comfort during photography
- Respect withdrawal immediately
- Thank them for participation

## Section 2: Required Elements of Consent

### Information That Must Be Shared

**1. Purpose**
- "This is for a WHO project"
- "We are training AI to diagnose skin conditions"
- "This will help doctors in the future"
- "Your photos help improve healthcare"

**2. What Will Happen**
- "I will take photographs of your skin condition"
- "The photos will be uploaded to a secure system"
- "Experts will review them for quality"
- "They will be used to train computer programs"
- "Your identity will be removed"

**3. Voluntary Participation**
- "You do not have to participate"
- "This will not affect your medical care"
- "You can say no at any time"
- "There is no penalty for refusing"

**4. Privacy and Confidentiality**
- "Your name will not be used"
- "We will remove identifying features"
- "Only authorized people will see the original"
- "Published images will be completely anonymous"
- "We follow strict data protection rules"

**5. Risks**
- "Minimal risk"
- "Possible discomfort during photography"
- "Rare: someone might recognize the location if it's unique"
- "We take steps to prevent this"

**6. Benefits**
- "You may not benefit directly"
- "Future patients may get better diagnosis"
- "Helps improve leprosy care globally"
- "Contributes to medical knowledge"

**7. Who to Contact**
- Provide your name
- Provide institution contact
- Provide ethics committee info
- Provide TLM contact

### Information Should Be:
- **Accurate**: No false promises
- **Complete**: Don't hide important details
- **Understandable**: Appropriate language level
- **Relevant**: Focus on what matters to patient

## Section 3: Special Consent Situations

### Language Barriers

**When Patient Speaks Different Language:**

**NEVER:**
- Use family members as interpreters for consent
- Proceed with gestures alone
- Assume understanding from nodding
- Use other patients as interpreters

**ALWAYS:**
- Use professional interpreters
- Use phone interpretation services
- Use translated consent forms
- Allow extra time
- Verify understanding

**Why Family Interpreters Are Problematic:**
- May not translate accurately
- May add their own opinions
- Patient may not feel free to refuse
- May hide information from patient
- Confidentiality concerns

**Options for Interpretation:**
1. In-person professional interpreter
2. Phone/video interpretation services
3. Bilingual staff member (if not family)
4. Translated written materials
5. Pictures/visual aids

### Low Literacy

**When Patient Cannot Read:**

- Read the consent form aloud
- Use verbal consent processes
- Use visual aids and diagrams
- Check understanding carefully
- Witness signature may be needed
- Audio recordings in some jurisdictions

**Verify Understanding:**
- Ask patient to explain back
- Use teach-back method
- Check for questions
- Observe for confusion

### Cognitive Impairment

**When Patient Has Reduced Capacity:**

**Assess:**
- Can they understand the information?
- Can they appreciate the consequences?
- Can they communicate a decision?
- Is the decision consistent?

**If Capacity in Doubt:**
- Consult with supervisor
- Involve family/caregivers (but they cannot consent for adult)
- Follow institutional protocols
- Document thoroughly
- May need to exclude if valid consent impossible

**Capacity is Decision-Specific:**
- Patient may understand simple consent even if they have some cognitive impairment
- Requires assessment, not assumption
- Guardians can consent for wards (legal arrangements)

## Section 4: Ongoing Consent

### Consent is Continuous

**Key Principle:** Consent is not a one-time event. It continues throughout the process.

**Checkpoints:**
1. **Initial discussion** - Information and questions
2. **Before photography** - Confirm still willing
3. **During photography** - Check comfort level
4. **Between photos** - Ask if okay to continue
5. **After photography** - Thank and remind of withdrawal rights

**Non-Verbal Cues to Watch For:**
- Looking away or avoiding eye contact
- Tense body language
- Fidgeting or restlessness
- Facial expressions of discomfort
- Hesitation or delay in responses
- Looking to others for cues

**Verbal Cues:**
- "I'm not sure..."
- "Do I have to...?"
- "How long will this take?"
- Questions that suggest worry
- Reduced engagement

**If You Observe Concern:**
1. Pause immediately
2. Ask if everything is okay
3. Address specific concerns
4. Offer to stop if they want
5. Re-explain if needed
6. Respect their decision

### Withdrawal of Consent

**Patient Can Withdraw at ANY Time:**
- Before photography starts
- During photography
- Between photos
- After photography (before upload)
- Even after submission (in some jurisdictions)

**When Patient Says "Stop":**
1. **Stop immediately** - No questions asked
2. **Thank them** - For what they allowed
3. **Confirm** - "So we're stopping now?"
4. **Document** - Note withdrawal in records
5. **Delete** - Any photos taken if requested
6. **No pressure** - Do not try to convince them
7. **Respect** - Their decision completely

**Responding to Withdrawal:**
- "Of course, we can stop."
- "Thank you for what you shared."
- "This doesn't affect your care."
- "You can change your mind later if you want."
- "Is there anything that would make you more comfortable?"

**Never:**
- Ask "Why?" in a challenging way
- Suggest they continue
- Show disappointment
- Make them feel guilty
- Pressure in any form

## Section 5: Recognizing Coercion and Undue Influence

### Signs Consent May Not Be Voluntary

**Red Flag 1: Confusion or Lack of Understanding**
- Patient seems confused about what they're agreeing to
- Cannot explain back what will happen
- Signs form without reading or asking questions
- Asks questions that suggest misunderstanding

**Red Flag 2: External Pressure**
- Looks to family member before answering
- Family member speaks for patient
- Patient seems fearful of family member
- Staff member pressuring participation

**Red Flag 3: Fear or Intimidation**
- Appears fearful or anxious
- Agrees too quickly
- Seems intimidated by authority
- Body language shows distress

**Red Flag 4: Vulnerability Exploitation**
- Very poor patient
- Socially marginalized
- Lacks support system
- May feel obligated to "help"

**Red Flag 5: Undue Benefit Promises**
- Expecting payment beyond reasonable compensation
- Expecting preferential treatment
- Believing participation guarantees cure
- Misunderstanding benefits

### Power Dynamics

**Be Aware of:**
- **Doctor-patient power imbalance** - You are the authority
- **Institutional power** - Organization reputation
- **Economic power** - If providing compensation
- **Gender dynamics** - In some cultures
- **Age dynamics** - Elders vs. youth

**Mitigate by:**
- Speaking as equals
- Emphasizing voluntary nature
- Creating safe space to refuse
- Using neutral language
- Offering alternatives

### Cultural Considerations

**Respect Cultural Differences:**
- Gender roles and interactions
- Concepts of privacy
- Decision-making patterns
- Communication styles
- Eye contact norms
- Touch boundaries

**Ask if Uncertain:**
- "Is it okay if I examine this area?"
- "Would you prefer a [male/female] clinician?"
- "Do you have any cultural concerns about this?"

## Section 6: Special Populations

### Children and Adolescents

**Legal Requirements:**
- Parent/guardian consent required
- Age of majority varies by country
- Some jurisdictions require both parents

**Assent (Child's Agreement):**
- In addition to parental consent
- Age-appropriate explanation
- Child can refuse even if parent consents
- Respect child's autonomy
- Use appropriate language for age

**Age Guidelines:**
- **Under 7**: Assent not required (but watch for distress)
- **7-12**: Obtain assent, simple explanation
- **13-17**: Full assent process, more detailed explanation
- **18+**: Adult consent process

**Child-Friendly Approach:**
- Get down to their eye level
- Use simple words
- Show them the camera
- Let them ask questions
- Make it a positive experience
- Have parent present

**If Child Refuses:**
- Stop immediately
- Respect their decision
- Thank them for considering
- Explain to parent
- Never force or pressure

### Elderly Patients

**Considerations:**
- May need more time
- May have hearing difficulties
- May have cognitive changes
- May defer to family
- May have different concepts of research

**Approach:**
- Speak clearly and slowly
- Allow extra time
- Check understanding frequently
- Respect autonomy
- Don't assume incapacity

### Patients with Disabilities

**Physical Disabilities:**
- Position comfortably
- Adapt photography approach
- Allow extra time
- Respect dignity

**Intellectual Disabilities:**
- Simple, clear language
- Visual aids
- Guardian consent
- Patient assent when possible
- Patience and repetition

**Sensory Impairments:**
- Visual: Verbal explanation, tactile aids
- Hearing: Written materials, sign language
- Ensure communication is effective

### Marginalized and Vulnerable Populations

**Populations Requiring Extra Protection:**
- People living in poverty
- Ethnic minorities
- Migrants and refugees
- People affected by stigma
- Those with limited education

**Additional Safeguards:**
- Community engagement
- Cultural liaisons
- Extra time for consent
- Ensure no exploitation
- Monitor for coercion

## Section 7: Documentation of Consent

### What to Document

**Required Documentation:**
1. **Date and time** of consent
2. **Who obtained** consent
3. **What information** was provided
4. **Patient questions** and responses
5. **Consent decision** (yes/no/withdrawn)
6. **Signature** or mark
7. **Witness** (if required)
8. **Copy given** to patient (if applicable)

**Electronic Consent Systems:**
- Digital signature or click-to-agree
- Timestamp automatically recorded
- IP address captured
- Audit trail maintained
- Same requirements as paper

### Consent Forms

**Paper Forms Should Include:**
- Project title and purpose
- Procedures explained
- Risks and benefits
- Voluntary participation statement
- Confidentiality protections
- Contact information
- Signature lines
- Date

**Electronic Forms Should:**
- Be user-friendly
- Allow review before submission
- Require active acceptance
- Provide copy to participant
- Be secure and encrypted

### When Consent is NOT Valid

**Do Not Proceed If:**
- Patient does not understand
- Consent appears coerced
- Patient lacks capacity (and no guardian)
- Required elements missing
- Patient withdrew consent
- Documentation incomplete

**If Valid Consent Cannot Be Obtained:**
- Do not include patient in project
- Document attempt
- Explain to patient (if appropriate)
- Note in records

## Section 8: Knowledge Check and Scenarios

### Scenario 1: The Interpreter

**Situation:** You need to photograph a patient's leg lesions. The patient speaks a language you don't understand. Their daughter offers to translate.

**What should you do?**

A) Accept the daughter's offer - family knows best
B) Wait for a professional interpreter or use phone service
C) Proceed with simple gestures - photography is universal
D) Ask the daughter to convince her parent to participate

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Professional interpretation is required for informed consent. Family members may not translate accurately, may add opinions, or the patient may not feel free to refuse. Phone interpretation services are an acceptable alternative.

### Scenario 2: The Hesitant Patient

**Situation:** During consent discussion for a back lesion photo, the patient keeps looking at their spouse before answering and seems nervous. When you ask if they have questions, they say "no" but seem uncertain.

**What should you do?**

A) Proceed - they said yes
B) Ask the spouse to leave and speak with patient privately
C) Ask them to sign quickly before they change their mind
D) Call it off entirely

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** These are red flags for possible coercion. Speaking privately allows the patient to express themselves freely. You can assess whether consent is truly voluntary.

### Scenario 3: Withdrawal

**Situation:** You have consent and have photographed one lesion. As you prepare to photograph a second lesion, the patient says "Actually, I changed my mind. I don't want to do this anymore."

**What should you do?**

A) Explain that you're almost done and encourage them to continue
B) Ask why they changed their mind and address concerns
C) Stop immediately and thank them
D) Suggest taking just one more photo

**Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Consent can be withdrawn at ANY time, even mid-procedure. You must stop immediately, respect their decision completely, and thank them for what they've provided.

### Scenario 4: The Child

**Situation:** A parent has consented for their 10-year-old child's photos. The child says "I don't want to" and looks uncomfortable.

**What should you do?**

A) Proceed because parent has legal authority
B) Stop - respect the child's wishes
C) Ask the parent to convince the child
D) Take the photos quickly before the child objects more

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** While parents provide legal consent, children should provide assent. If they refuse or are uncomfortable, respect their bodily autonomy. Never force a child to be photographed against their will.

### Multiple Choice Questions

**Question 1:** Which is NOT a required element of informed consent?

A) Purpose of the project
B) Voluntary nature
C) Guarantee of payment
D) Privacy protections

**Answer:** C

**Question 2:** True or False: Consent is a one-time event that happens before photography begins.

**Answer:** False

**Explanation:** Consent is an ongoing process that continues throughout. You should check in regularly and respect withdrawal at any time.

**Question 3:** A patient has limited literacy and cannot read the consent form. What should you do?

A) Have a family member read it to them
B) Read it aloud and verify understanding
C) Skip the written form
D) Find a different patient

**Answer:** B

**Explanation:** Read the form aloud, use verbal consent processes, and verify understanding using teach-back methods.

## Section 9: Module Summary

### Key Principles

**1. Informed consent requires:**
   - Information
   - Comprehension
   - Voluntariness
   - Capacity

**2. Consent is continuous:**
   - Check throughout process
   - Watch for non-verbal cues
   - Respect withdrawal immediately

**3. Red flags for non-voluntary consent:**
   - Looking to others for approval
   - Confusion or fear
   - Agreeing too quickly
   - Signs of coercion

**4. Special populations need extra care:**
   - Children: Assent + parental consent
   - Language barriers: Professional interpreters
   - Vulnerable: Additional safeguards

**5. Documentation is essential:**
   - Record who, when, what
   - Maintain secure records
   - Provide copies if required

### Consent Checklist

Before photographing:
- [ ] Purpose explained clearly
- [ ] Risks and benefits discussed
- [ ] Voluntary nature emphasized
- [ ] Questions answered
- [ ] Understanding verified
- [ ] Consent documented
- [ ] Privacy ensured
- [ ] No signs of coercion
- [ ] Ongoing consent monitored

### Remember

**Golden Rules:**
1. **When in doubt, STOP**
2. **Patient welfare over project needs**
3. **Consent can end at any moment**
4. **No coercion, ever**
5. **Document everything**

**Red Flags = STOP and Reassess**
