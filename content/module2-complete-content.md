## Comprehensive Content Document

### Learning Objectives
By the end of this module, participants will be able to:
1. Capture high-quality clinical images suitable for AI training
2. Complete all 10 required metadata fields accurately
3. Apply appropriate photography techniques for different clinical scenarios
4. Handle sensitive body areas with professionalism and dignity
5. Avoid common photography mistakes

---

## Section 1: Introduction to Clinical Photography

### Why Image Quality Matters
The effectiveness of AI diagnostic systems depends directly on the quality of training data. Poor quality images:
- Reduce AI accuracy and reliability
- Waste valuable clinical time
- May be rejected from the dataset
- Cannot be used for training

High-quality images enable AI to:
- Learn visual patterns accurately
- Distinguish between conditions
- Provide reliable diagnostic support
- Work across diverse populations

### The Clinical Photography Mindset
When photographing for this project:
1. **Think diagnostically**: What would a dermatologist need to see?
2. **Think inclusively**: Does this represent the diversity needed?
3. **Think ethically**: Am I maintaining patient dignity?
4. **Think systematically**: Am I following the required process?

## Section 2: The 10 Required Metadata Fields

Every image submission must include these mandatory metadata fields:

### Field 1: Partner Organization Name
- **Type**: Dropdown (pre-populated)
- **Required**: Yes
- **Purpose**: Tracks which organization contributed the image
- **Example**: "TLM Bangladesh", "ALERT Ethiopia", "Damien Foundation India"

### Field 2: Submitting Clinician Name
- **Type**: Text entry
- **Required**: Yes
- **Purpose**: Identifies the clinician for attribution and verification
- **Note**: Your name will not be publicly associated with specific images

### Field 3: Country
- **Type**: Dropdown
- **Required**: Yes
- **Purpose**: Ensures geographic diversity for AI training
- **Importance**: AI must work across different regions and populations

### Field 4: Clinical Form (Ridley-Jopling Classification)
- **Type**: Dropdown
- **Required**: Yes
- **Options**:
  - TT (Tuberculoid)
  - BT (Borderline Tuberculoid)
  - BB (Borderline)
  - BL (Borderline Lepromatous)
  - LL (Lepromatous)
  - Pure Neural
  - Indeterminate
  - Reaction State
- **Purpose**: Classifies leprosy type for AI training

### Field 5: Operational Classification
- **Type**: Dropdown
- **Required**: Yes
- **Options**:
  - PB (Paucibacillary)
  - MB (Multibacillary)
- **Purpose**: Treatment category classification
- **Impact**: Affects treatment duration and regimen

### Field 6: Reaction Status
- **Type**: Dropdown
- **Required**: Yes
- **Options**:
  - No reaction
  - Type 1 reaction
  - Type 2 reaction (ENL)
  - Lucio phenomenon
  - Not determined
- **Purpose**: Identifies inflammatory complications

### Field 7: Gender
- **Type**: Dropdown
- **Required**: Yes
- **Options**: Male, Female
- **Purpose**: Ensures gender balance in training data

### Field 8: Age Group
- **Type**: Dropdown
- **Required**: Yes
- **Options**:
  - Under 5 years
  - 5-14 years
  - 15-24 years
  - 25-34 years
  - 35-44 years
  - 45-54 years
  - 55-64 years
  - 65+ years
- **Purpose**: Ensures age diversity in dataset

### Field 9: Phototype (Fitzpatrick Scale)
- **Type**: Dropdown
- **Required**: Yes
- **Options**:
  - Type I: Very light, always burns
  - Type II: Light, usually burns
  - Type III: Medium, sometimes burns
  - Type IV: Olive, rarely burns
  - Type V: Brown, very rarely burns
  - Type VI: Dark brown/black, never burns
- **Purpose**: CRITICAL for ensuring AI works across all skin tones
- **Note**: This is about skin response to sun, not race or ethnicity

### Field 10: Additional Clinical Notes
- **Type**: Free text
- **Required**: No (but encouraged)
- **Purpose**: Additional context that may help AI training
- **Examples**: "Multiple lesions", "Facial involvement", "Previous treatment"

### Additional Field: Anatomical Location
- **Type**: Dropdown/Multiple select
- **Required**: Yes
- **Options**: Head/neck, upper extremities, trunk, lower extremities, genitalia, other
- **Purpose**: Links image to specific body location
- **Note**: Required for each image submitted

## Section 3: Image Quality Standards

### Technical Requirements

**Resolution:**
- Minimum: 1920 x 1080 pixels (Full HD)
- Preferred: Higher resolution if available
- File formats: JPEG (compressed), PNG (uncompressed)

**Focus:**
- Lesion must be in sharp focus
- Use macro mode for close-ups
- Check focus before submission
- Retake if blurry

**Lighting:**
- Use natural or clinical lighting
- Avoid harsh shadows
- Prevent overexposure (too bright)
- Prevent underexposure (too dark)
- Consistent lighting across image

**Framing:**
- Center the lesion in frame
- Include surrounding healthy skin for context
- Use portrait orientation for vertical lesions
- Use landscape for horizontal lesions
- Leave appropriate margins

### Quality Checklist
Before submitting any image, verify:
- [ ] Image is in focus
- [ ] Lighting is adequate and even
- [ ] No motion blur
- [ ] Lesion is clearly visible
- [ ] Surrounding skin is visible
- [ ] Scale reference included (if needed)
- [ ] No identifying information visible
- [ ] Appropriate orientation
- [ ] High enough resolution

## Section 4: Photography Best Practices

### Equipment Preparation

**Camera Selection:**
- Smartphone cameras (modern, high-quality)
- Digital cameras
- Clinic cameras with macro capability
- Avoid: Webcams, low-resolution cameras

**Before You Start:**
1. Clean camera lens with cloth
2. Check battery level
3. Ensure adequate storage space
4. Set to highest resolution
5. Enable grid lines (if available)
6. Turn off flash (usually)

### Photography Techniques

**The RIGHT Way:**

1. **Position the Patient**
   - Ensure comfortable position
   - Adequate support for body part
   - Privacy maintained
   - Good access to lesion

2. **Prepare the Area**
   - Clean skin if needed
   - Remove bandages (if clinically appropriate)
   - Ensure adequate lighting
   - Use neutral background (drape/sheet)

3. **Set Up the Shot**
   - Position camera perpendicular to lesion
   - Distance: 10-30 cm for most lesions
   - Use macro mode for detail
   - Include scale if size is important

4. **Capture Multiple Views**
   - Wide shot showing location
   - Close-up of lesion detail
   - Different angles if needed
   - Before/after if applicable

5. **Check Immediately**
   - Review images on screen
   - Verify focus and lighting
   - Confirm no identifying features
   - Retake if necessary

**Common Scale References:**
- Ruler (preferred)
- Coin (local currency)
- Credit card
- Finger width

## Section 5: What to Photograph

### Essential Shots

**For Each Lesion, Capture:**
1. **Context shot**: Shows location on body
2. **Detailed shot**: Close-up of lesion characteristics
3. **Surrounding skin**: Shows contrast with healthy tissue

### Lesion Characteristics to Capture

**Document these features:**
- Size and shape
- Color and pigmentation
- Surface texture
- Borders/edges
- Distribution pattern
- Associated features (ulceration, discharge, hair loss)

**Multiple Lesions:**
- If patient has multiple lesions, photograph:
  - The most representative lesion
  - The most severe lesion
  - Different types if present
  - Each body area separately

## Section 6: What NOT to Include

### Identifying Information (MUST REMOVE)

**Patient Identifiers:**
- Face or facial features
- Names on wristbands or clothing
- Tattoos (unless lesion itself)
- Unique birthmarks
- Scars that could identify patient
- Jewelry with initials

**Location Identifiers:**
- Hospital room numbers
- Institutional logos
- Window views with landmarks
- Name tags or badges
- Equipment with serial numbers

**Other Identifiers:**
- Dates on documents
- Handwriting
- Reflections showing people
- Background clutter

### Technical Problems (AVOID)

**Common Mistakes:**
1. **Out of focus** - Check before submission
2. **Poor lighting** - Too dark or washed out
3. **Motion blur** - Patient moved during shot
4. **Wrong angle** - Photographed from side instead of top
5. **Too far** - Can't see detail
6. **Too close** - Lost context
7. **Flash glare** - Reflection obscures lesion
8. **Shadows** - Uneven lighting

## Section 7: Special Considerations

### Photography of Private Body Areas

**When lesions are on breasts, genitals, buttocks, or inner thighs:**

**Additional Requirements:**
1. **Specific Consent**
   - Obtain explicit consent for that body region
   - Explain why that area needs photography
   - Document separately if required

2. **Privacy Measures**
   - Close doors and curtains
   - Minimal personnel present
   - Gender concordance when possible
   - Offer chaperone of patient's choice

3. **Professional Draping**
   - Expose only necessary area
   - Use towels/sheets strategically
   - Maintain patient's modesty
   - Work efficiently

4. **Technical Approach**
   - Photograph only the lesion area
   - No full-body shots
   - Careful framing to avoid adjacent areas
   - Immediate deletion of unsuccessful shots

**After Photography:**
- Allow patient to redress in privacy
- Thank them for their cooperation
- Maintain professional demeanor
- No inappropriate comments

### Children

**For pediatric patients:**
- Parent/guardian must be present
- Obtain assent from child (if age appropriate)
- Extra gentleness and patience
- Distract with toys or conversation
- Consider child's comfort above all

### Vulnerable Populations

**Extra sensitivity for:**
- Elderly patients
- Patients with disabilities
- Cognitively impaired patients
- Those from marginalized communities
- Patients with mental health conditions

**Approach:**
- Extra time for explanation
- Simple, clear language
- Frequent reassurance
- Respect for autonomy
- Institutional support if needed

## Section 8: Image Submission Process

### Step-by-Step Workflow

1. **Take Photographs**
   - Follow techniques above
   - Capture multiple angles
   - Check quality immediately

2. **Review and Select**
   - Choose best images
   - Delete poor quality shots
   - Ensure adequate representation

3. **Anonymize**
   - Crop out identifying features
   - Remove metadata (location data, etc.)
   - Verify no patient info visible

4. **Complete Metadata**
   - Fill all 10 required fields
   - Double-check accuracy
   - Add clinical notes if relevant

5. **Upload Securely**
   - Use authorized upload system only
   - Verify upload successful
   - Do not email or message images
   - Delete from device after upload

6. **Document**
   - Record submission in patient notes
   - Note any issues or concerns
   - Keep personal records as required

### Security Protocols

**DO:**
- Upload only through secure system
- Delete images from phone after upload
- Use encrypted connections
- Follow institutional IT policies
- Report security concerns

**DON'T:**
- Email images
- Use messaging apps (WhatsApp, SMS)
- Store on personal cloud accounts
- Share with colleagues outside project
- Post on social media
- Keep images on device long-term

## Section 9: Troubleshooting Common Problems

### Problem: Images are blurry
**Solutions:**
- Clean camera lens
- Use both hands to steady
- Ask patient to hold position
- Increase lighting
- Use camera's stabilization
- Take multiple shots

### Problem: Colors look wrong
**Solutions:**
- Use natural light when possible
- Avoid mixed lighting (fluorescent + window)
- Check white balance settings
- Photograph on neutral background

### Problem: Can't get close enough
**Solutions:**
- Use macro mode
- Get physically closer
- Use zoom after ensuring focus
- Consider different camera

### Problem: Patient uncomfortable
**Solutions:**
- Take breaks
- Explain each step
- Use chaperone if helpful
- Consider rescheduling
- Never rush or pressure

### Problem: Background is cluttered
**Solutions:**
- Use plain drape or sheet
- Change position
- Zoom in to crop background
- Clear area before starting

## Section 10: Knowledge Check

**Question 1:** Which metadata field is used to ensure skin tone diversity in the AI training dataset?

A) Age Group
B) Clinical Form (Ridley-Jopling Classification)
C) Phototype (Fitzpatrick Scale)
D) Operational Classification

**Correct Answer:** C
**Explanation:** The Fitzpatrick Scale (I-VI) documents skin tone to ensure AI works across diverse populations. This is critical for equitable AI performance.

**Question 2:** True or False: You should use the camera flash for all clinical photographs.

**Correct Answer:** False
**Explanation:** Flash often creates glare and harsh shadows. Natural or clinical lighting is preferred. Flash should be avoided unless specifically needed for dark areas.

**Question 3:** When photographing lesions on private body areas, which is required?

A) A full-body photograph for context
B) Explicit consent specifically for that body region
C) Photography with multiple staff members present
D) Standard consent form is sufficient

**Correct Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Private areas require explicit, specific consent. General consent is insufficient. Full-body photos should be avoided, privacy should be maximized, and staff presence minimized.

**Question 4:** Which of the following should be included in clinical photos?

A) Patient's hospital wristband
B) Clinical scale or ruler
C) Patient's face for identification
D) Tattoo on patient's arm near lesion

**Correct Answer:** B
**Explanation:** A ruler or scale is helpful for size reference. Wristbands, faces, and tattoos (unless the tattoo IS the lesion) should be removed or covered.

**Question 5:** How many required metadata fields must be completed for each image submission?

A) 5
B) 8
C) 10
D) 15

**Correct Answer:** C
**Explanation:** There are 10 mandatory metadata fields plus anatomical location that must be completed for every image submission.

**Question 6:** What is the minimum recommended image resolution?

A) 640 x 480 pixels
B) 1280 x 720 pixels
C) 1920 x 1080 pixels
D) 3840 x 2160 pixels

**Correct Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Full HD (1920 x 1080) is the minimum. Higher resolution is preferred if available.

**Question 7:** After uploading images to the secure system, what should you do?

A) Keep copies on your phone for reference
B) Email them to your supervisor
C) Delete them from your device
D) Post on professional social media

**Correct Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Delete images from your device after secure upload. Never email, message, or post images. Maintain security at all times.
