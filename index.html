Module 1: Project Overview & Objectives

Learning Objectives: By the end of this module, you will understand the WHO SkinNTD AI project, the governance structure, your role as a contributing clinician, and the ethical principles guiding this initiative.

1.1 Why This Project Matters

The Challenge: Leprosy remains underdiagnosed in many regions worldwide. Despite being curable, delayed diagnosis leads to permanent disabilities, social stigma, continued transmission, and economic hardship for affected families.

The Role of AI: Artificial Intelligence can help bridge the diagnostic gap by assisting primary health workers who lack specialist training, providing consistent diagnostic support across regions, enabling earlier detection and treatment, and reducing the burden on scarce dermatology specialists.

The Need for Quality Data: AI systems learn from examples. To create an effective diagnostic tool, we need thousands of diverse clinical images from different skin tones (Fitzpatrick types I-VI), various geographic regions, different disease stages, and properly anonymized and consented images.

Your Contribution: As a contributing clinician, you play a critical role in this global health initiative. Each high-quality image you submit helps train AI systems that will benefit patients in underserved communities, support healthcare workers with limited resources, and contribute to the global effort to eliminate leprosy.

1.2 Understanding the AI Training Process

How AI Learns from Images: Machine learning systems analyze visual patterns in thousands of clinical images, learn correlations between visual features and diagnoses, build predictive models, and improve accuracy through exposure to diverse examples.

What Makes a Good Training Image:

  • Clinically informative: Shows the condition clearly
  • Properly labeled: Includes accurate metadata and diagnosis
  • Anonymized: Contains no identifying patient information
  • Diverse: Represents different populations and presentations
  • High quality: In focus, well-lit, properly framed

1.3 Governance Structure

The WHO SkinNTD AI Image Collection Initiative operates through a four-level governance structure:

Level Parties Responsibilities
Level 1 WHO - TLM International Strategic oversight, ethical standards, licensing, global health alignment
Level 2 TLM - Partner Organizations Partnership agreements, training, compliance, data security
Level 3 Partner Org - Clinicians Authorization, institutional support, quality control
Level 4 Clinician - Patient Direct patient care, informed consent, dignity, clinical judgment
Your Position: You are at Level 4 with direct responsibility to patients. You have the authority to photograph eligible patients but must follow institutional protocols and maintain your ethical obligations to patients. Patient welfare always comes first.

1.4 Project Objectives

Primary Objective: To build a diverse, high-quality dataset of clinical images of leprosy and skin NTDs for training AI diagnostic systems for deployment in resource-limited healthcare settings.

Specific Goals:

  • Collect 10,000+ images across diverse populations
  • Ensure geographic diversity from all WHO regions
  • Represent all skin phototypes (Fitzpatrick I-VI)
  • Include multiple disease presentations and stages
  • Maintain highest ethical standards throughout

1.6 Ethical Principles

The project is guided by four core ethical principles:

  1. Respect for Persons: Autonomy, dignity, and extra protection for vulnerable individuals
  2. Beneficence: Benefit patients through improved diagnosis and contribute to global health
  3. Non-maleficence: Do no harm, protect from stigma, prevent privacy breaches, avoid exploitation

Module 2: Clinical Photography Best Practices

Learning Objectives: By the end of this module, you will be able to capture high-quality clinical images suitable for AI training and accurately complete all 10 required metadata fields.

2.1 Why Image Quality Matters

The effectiveness of AI diagnostic systems depends directly on the quality of training data. High-quality images enable AI to distinguish between conditions and work across diverse populations accurately.

2.2 The 10 Required Metadata Fields

Every image submission must include these mandatory fields:

  • 1. Partner Organization: Tracks the contributing organization.
  • 2. Clinician Name: Identifies the clinician (for verification).
  • 3. Country: Ensures geographic diversity.
  • 4. Clinical Form: Ridley-Jopling classification (TT, BT, BB, BL, LL, etc.).
  • 5. Operational Classification: PB (Paucibacillary) or MB (Multibacillary).
  • 6. Reaction Status: Identifies inflammatory complications (Type 1, Type 2, etc.).
  • 7. Gender: Ensures gender balance in data.
  • 8. Age Group: Ensures age diversity.
  • 9. Phototype (Fitzpatrick Scale): CRITICAL for ensuring AI works across all skin tones (Types I-VI).
  • 10. Additional Notes: Context like "multiple lesions" or "facial involvement".

2.3 Technical Requirements

Quality Checklist

Mandatory Removal: You MUST remove or obscure all identifying information: faces, names on wristbands, tattoos, jewelry, and hospital logos.

2.4 Knowledge Check

Question 1: Which metadata field is used to ensure skin tone diversity?

Correct! The Fitzpatrick Scale (I-VI) documents skin response to the sun, ensuring the AI performs equitably across all skin types.

Module 3: Informed Consent Procedures

Learning Objectives: Learn the elements of valid informed consent, how to navigate language barriers, and how to respond professionally to the withdrawal of consent.

3.1 The 4 Pillars of Consent

Valid informed consent requires more than a signature; it is a process involving:

  • Information: Clear explanation of purpose (WHO AI training).
  • Comprehension: Ensuring the patient truly understands the request.
  • Voluntariness: Freedom to decline without penalty or pressure.
  • Capacity: Ability to make an informed decision (not distressed or impaired).

Scenario: Language Barriers

If a patient speaks a language you don't understand, NEVER use family members as interpreters for consent. Always use professional interpreters or phone services to ensure accurate, confidential, and pressure-free communication.

3.2 Continuous Consent

Consent is not a one-time event. You must check in with the patient:

  1. Before the first photograph.
  2. Between captures (especially for different body areas).
  3. Immediately if you notice non-verbal cues of distress (tensing up, looking away).
When to STOP: If a patient says "Stop" or "I've changed my mind," you must stop immediately, thank them, and never pressure them to continue. This will NOT affect their medical care.

3.3 Knowledge Check

Question: A parent has consented for their 10-year-old child, but the child says "I don't want to." What should you do?

Correct! While parents provide legal consent, children should provide assent. If they refuse, respect their bodily autonomy. Never force a child to be photographed.

Module 4: Anonymization & Privacy Protection

Learning Objectives: Identify and remove all personal identifiers from clinical images to protect patient privacy and prevent stigma.

4.1 The Anonymization Rule

Protecting the patient's identity is an ethical and legal mandate. Even with consent, you must remove features that could link the photo to a human being.

What to Remove (The Big Five):

  1. Faces: Entire face, eyes, or ears.
  2. Text: Hospital wristbands, name tags, or handwritten notes.
  3. Unique Features: Tattoos (if distinctive), birthmarks (not related to lesion), or jewelry.
  4. Logos: Institutional logos on gowns or bedsheets.
  5. Metadata: EXIF data (GPS coordinates) attached to digital files.

Anonymization Techniques

Security Protocol: Delete images from your personal phone or device immediately after they are securely uploaded to the authorized system. Never share images via WhatsApp, SMS, or social media.

4.2 Knowledge Check

Question: Which of these should NOT be removed from a clinical photo?

Correct! The lesion is the subject of the study. All other items are potential identifiers that must be removed.

Module 5: Safeguarding Principles

Learning Objectives: Identify safeguarding concerns and fulfill your mandatory reporting duties as a healthcare provider.

5.1 The Principles of Dignity

Safeguarding ensures that patients live free from harm, abuse, and neglect.

  • Respect for Persons: Treat everyone with inherent worth. Use private spaces and respectful communication.
  • Sensitive Body Areas: Genitalia, breasts, or buttocks require explicit consent, minimal personnel, and professional draping.
  • Vulnerability: Provide extra protection for children, the elderly, and those from marginalized communities.
Mandatory Reporting: If you suspect child abuse, elder abuse, or domestic violence during your examination, you must follow your institution's reporting protocols immediately. Safety takes precedence over the AI project.

When to STOP:

If you observe signs of coercion (e.g., a patient looking to a spouse for permission repeatedly) or fear, STOP the process and speak to the patient privately. Do not submit photos if you doubt consent was voluntary.

5.2 Knowledge Check

Question: You notice unexplained bruises on a child during photography. What is your priority?

Correct! Patient safety is always the first priority. Suspected abuse must be reported per local laws and institutional policy.
